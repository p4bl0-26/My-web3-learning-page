Hi! I am learning about Web3. Here is a glossary of words I am learning. I added a filter and dark mode!

Basics

Web3

The "third generation" of the internet. It's an idea for a new internet built on blockchains, where users have more control and ownership over their data.

Learn more | Watch video

Bitcoin

The very first cryptocurrency. It's a form of digital money that is created and held electronically. No single person or bank controls it.

Learn more | Watch video

Ethereum

A global platform for decentralized applications (dApps). While Bitcoin is just a currency, Ethereum is a network that can run "smart contracts."

Learn more | Watch video

Decentralization

The idea that control and decision-making are spread out among many users, rather than being controlled by one single person, company, or government.

Learn more | Watch video

Ledger

A digital file or book that records all transactions. On a blockchain, this ledger is public and shared across many computers, making it very hard to change.

Learn more | Watch video

Fiat Currency

Government-issued money that is not backed by a physical thing (like gold). Examples are the US Dollar, Euro, or Indian Rupee. This is what crypto is an alternative to.

Learn more | Watch video

Blockchain

A blockchain is a growing list of records, called blocks, that are linked together using cryptography. It's like a digital notebook that is shared and can't be changed.

Learn more | Watch video

Cryptocurrency

A digital token. It's a digital currency that lets people make payments directly to each other online without a bank. Bitcoin is the most famous one.

Learn more | Watch video

Crypto Wallet

A digital tool that lets you store and manage your cryptocurrencies. It holds your 'private keys' (like a password) that prove you own your crypto.

Learn more | Watch video

Core Concepts

Public Key

Your 'public' address. It's like your bank account number. You can share it with anyone to receive crypto. It's generated from your private key.

Learn more | Watch video

Private Key

The secret password to your crypto. It proves you own your funds and lets you send them. You must NEVER share this with anyone.

Learn more | Watch video

Seed Phrase

A list of 12 or 24 simple words that acts as a backup for your entire crypto wallet. If you lose your wallet, you can get it back with this phrase. Keep it super safe and offline.

Learn more | Watch video

Consensus Mechanism

The rules a blockchain uses to agree on which new transactions are real. It's how all the computers in the network agree on the "truth" without trusting each other.

Learn more | Watch video

Proof-of-Work (PoW)

The consensus mechanism used by Bitcoin. "Miners" use powerful computers to solve a hard puzzle. The first one to solve it gets to add the next block and is rewarded.

Learn more | Watch video

Proof-of-Stake (PoS)

A popular alternative to PoW. "Validators" lock up their own crypto (a "stake") to get a chance to add the next block. It's much more energy-efficient.

Learn more | Watch video

Mining

The process of creating new bitcoins (or other PoW coins) by solving complex puzzles with computers. This is also how new transactions are added to the blockchain.

Learn more | Watch video

Node

A computer that connects to the blockchain network. It helps validate transactions and keep the network secure by holding a copy of the ledger.

Learn more | Watch video

Token vs. Coin

A "Coin" (like Bitcoin) runs on its own blockchain. A "Token" (like UNI) is built on top of an existing blockchain, like Ethereum.

Learn more | Watch video

Governance Token

A special token that gives you voting rights in a dApp or DAO. The more tokens you have, the more power your vote has on new proposals.

Learn more | Watch video

Smart Contract

A computer program stored on a blockchain. It's an agreement in code that automatically runs when certain conditions are met. Think of it like a digital vending machine.

Learn more | Watch video

dApp (Decentralized Application)

A 'decentralized application'. It's like a normal app, but it runs on a blockchain using smart contracts. This means it isn't controlled by one single person or company.

Learn more | Watch video

DAO (Decentralized Autonomous Org)

An organization managed by its members and run by code on a blockchain. There is no central boss; decisions are made by voting with tokens.

Learn more | Watch video

Gas Fees

The transaction cost users pay to execute operations on a blockchain. You pay 'gas' to have miners or validators process and approve your transaction (like sending crypto).

Learn more | Watch video

Finance (DeFi)

Liquidity Pool

A big pot of two different tokens locked in a smart contract. These pools are what allow you to instantly swap tokens on a Decentralized Exchange (DEX).

Learn more | Watch video

Yield Farming

The practice of lending or staking your crypto in a DeFi app to earn rewards. People "farm" for the highest "yield" (reward rate) by moving their money around.

Learn more | Watch video

AMM (Automated Market Maker)

The smart contract that runs a DEX. It uses a math formula and a liquidity pool to set the price for tokens automatically, without needing a traditional "order book".

Learn more | Watch video

Stablecoin

A type of cryptocurrency whose value is "pegged" to a real-world asset, usually the US dollar. This keeps its price stable (e.g., 1 USDC = $1).

Learn more | Watch video

Oracle

A service that brings real-world information (like stock prices or weather) onto the blockchain. Smart contracts need oracles to work with data that isn't on the chain.

Learn more | Watch video

DeFi (Decentralized Finance)

A peer-to-peer system of financial tools (like lending or trading) built on a blockchain, without needing to use traditional banks.

Learn more | Watch video

DEX (Decentralized Exchange)

A peer-to-peer marketplace where transactions occur directly between crypto traders. There is no central company holding the funds (like Coinbase or Binance do).

Learn more | Watch video

Staking

The process of locking up your cryptocurrency for a period of time to help support the operation and security of a blockchain network. In return, you earn rewards (more crypto).

Learn more | Watch video

NFTs & Metaverse

Minting

The process of creating a new NFT. "Minting" is when you publish your digital art or item onto the blockchain, turning it into an NFT that can be bought or sold.

Learn more | Watch video

Airdrop

A free giveaway of tokens or NFTs to a large number of people. Projects do this to get attention, find new users, or reward their early supporters.

Learn more | Watch video

PFP (Profile Picture)

A very popular type of NFT. These are collections of thousands of unique characters (like CryptoPunks or Bored Apes) that people use as their profile pictures on social media.

Learn more | Watch video

NFT Marketplace

A website where you can buy, sell, or mint NFTs. The biggest one on Ethereum is called OpenSea.

Learn more | Watch video

NFT (Non-Fungible Token)

Stands for 'non-fungible token'. 'Non-fungible' means it's unique and can't be replaced. It's a digital certificate on a blockchain that proves you own a unique digital item, like art or a video.

Learn more | Watch video

Metaverse

A virtual world where users, represented by avatars, can interact, socialize, work, and play. Many metaverses use blockchain to let users own and trade digital land and items as NFTs.

Learn more | Watch video

Technology

Layer 1 (L1)

The main, base-level blockchain, like Bitcoin or Ethereum. These networks are secure but can sometimes be slow and expensive.

Learn more | Watch video

Layer 2 (L2)

A separate blockchain that runs on top of a main blockchain (a 'Layer 1' like Ethereum). Layer 2s are designed to be faster and have lower gas fees, making the network more efficient.

Learn more | Watch video

Rollups

A popular type of Layer 2. They "roll up" hundreds of transactions into a single batch and send just that one batch to the Layer 1, saving a lot of money on gas fees.

Learn more | Watch video

Blockchain Bridge

A tool that connects two different blockchains, allowing you to send tokens or data from one chain to another (e.g., from Ethereum to Polygon).

Learn more | Watch video

IPFS (InterPlanetary File System)

A decentralized system for storing files. Instead of a file living on one server (like "google.com/image.jpg"), it's stored in pieces across many computers. Many NFTs store their art on IPFS.

Learn more | Watch video

EVM (Ethereum Virtual Machine)

The "brain" of Ethereum that runs the smart contracts. It's the computer inside the network that does all the calculations. Many other blockchains are "EVM-compatible," meaning they can run the same code.

Learn more | Watch video

Ecosystem

MetaMask

The most popular crypto wallet for Ethereum and EVM chains. It's a browser extension or mobile app that lets you connect to dApps, send transactions, and manage your assets.

Learn more | Watch video

Solana

A popular Layer 1 blockchain that is known for being extremely fast and very cheap. It is a major competitor to Ethereum but works differently.

Learn more | Watch video

Polygon (Matic)

A popular Layer 2 scaling solution for Ethereum. It's a "sidechain" that is fast and cheap, making it very popular for dApps and NFTs.

Learn more | Watch video

Security

Hot Wallet

A crypto wallet that is connected to the internet (like MetaMask or a phone app). It's convenient for daily use, but less secure.

Learn more | Watch video

Cold Wallet (Hardware Wallet)

A physical device (like a USB drive) that stores your private keys completely offline. This is the safest way to store a large amount of crypto.

Learn more | Watch video

Phishing

A scam where someone tricks you into giving them your private key or seed phrase, often by making a fake website that looks real.

Learn more | Watch video

Rug Pull

A scam where the developers of a new crypto project abandon it and run away with all the investors' money.

Learn more | Watch video

Smart Contract Audit

A process where a security company checks the code of a smart contract for bugs or security holes before it's released. It's like a safety inspection.

Learn more | Watch video
